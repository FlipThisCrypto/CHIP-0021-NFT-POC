# Heartwood — an algorithmic philosophy

*The generative aesthetic behind The Orchard's Living Tree NFTs.*

## The movement

**Heartwood** is the belief that an image can be *earned* rather than *authored* — that a picture can be the honest residue of a physical thing surviving in the world. A Heartwood tree is never drawn; it is *grown*, frame by frame, only as fast as a real sensor node proves it deserves. Nothing in the composition is decorative. Every pixel is a claim the hardware has already made and the Oracle has already checked. The art is not a portrait of the device — it *is* the device's life, made legible.

## Computational expression

A Heartwood tree is a pure function of two things: an immutable **seed** (the device's cryptographic DNA) and an append-only **state vector** (what it has earned). The seed is fed through a deterministic hash-based PRNG — never `Math.random()` — so the same device always grows the same tree, on any machine, forever. The PRNG fixes the *structure*: species, the lean of the trunk, the scatter of leaf clusters, the slots where fruit may one day hang. The state vector then *modulates* that fixed skeleton — fullness, saturation, resolution, ornament — but can never reshape it. This is the discipline that separates Heartwood from a thousand random PFP engines: **structure is identity and is frozen; state is history and is additive.** Change a slider and you do not get a different tree — you get the *same* tree on a different day.

## Composite, never enumerate

The temptation with nine independent traits is to pre-render their product — and drown in millions of frozen frames. Heartwood refuses. It composites in strict z-order from a small set of primitives: roots, trunk, canopy, fruit, tag, halo, badges, weather. Each layer reads exactly one fact and writes to exactly one visual channel and one spatial zone, so the layers can never fight for the viewer's eye. Resolution itself is a channel: a seedling renders on a coarse 22-pixel grid, an ancient tree on a fine one, the nearest-neighbour upscale turning *age into literal sharpness*. Matter — trunk, leaves, fruit — is rendered as honest pixels; energy — the GPS root-glow, the legendary halo — is rendered as soft light. The eye learns the grammar in seconds and never unlearns it.

## The quiet seed

Beneath the system runs a single borrowed idea, woven in so it is felt rather than announced: **dendrochronology** — the reading of a tree's entire life from the record it cannot help but keep. A botanist needs no documentation to date a trunk; the rings testify. Heartwood asks the same of a machine. And the name itself is the pun that holds the whole project: *heartwood* is the dense inner core a tree lays down season after season from the simple fact of having lived — the visible sum of every **heartbeat**. A node that sends its signed heartbeats lays down heartwood. A node that goes silent lays down none. Those who know will catch it; everyone else simply sees a tree that is obviously, wordlessly alive.

## Craftsmanship

This must read as the product of deep computational expertise — a meticulously crafted engine, every threshold and proportion refined through countless iterations by someone at the top of their field, not a sketch that happens to run. The mastery lives in restraint: the painstaking calibration that lets health and season both touch the leaves *without ever being mistaken for each other* (health speaks in fullness and saturation; season speaks in hue and falling tokens); the master-level decision that a dormant winter tree and a dead one must never be confused (one keeps bright branches under blue snow, the other greys and breaks). Every parameter is load-bearing. Every default is a considered judgement. The result should feel inevitable — as though the tree could not have grown any other way — which is the highest praise a generative system can earn, and the only standard Heartwood accepts.
